Bankruptcy Exemptions - What Property You Can Keep

Exemptions protect certain property from liquidation in Chapter 7 and determine what creditors receive in Chapter 13. Understanding your exemptions is the most important step in bankruptcy planning.

What Are Bankruptcy Exemptions?

When you file bankruptcy, exemptions are the legal rules that determine which of your assets are protected from creditors. The word "exempt" literally means "taken out" - exempt property is taken out of the bankruptcy estate and cannot be sold to pay debts.

In Chapter 7, the bankruptcy trustee can sell your non-exempt property to pay creditors. Everything that falls within an exemption is yours to keep. If all your property is exempt - which is the case for most filers - the trustee has nothing to sell and your case is a "no-asset" case.

In Chapter 13, you keep all your property, but your plan payments must provide unsecured creditors at least as much as they would have received if your non-exempt assets had been liquidated in Chapter 7. This is called the "liquidation test" or "best interests of creditors" test under 11 U.S.C. Section 1325(a)(4).

The Bankruptcy Code provides a set of federal exemptions under 11 U.S.C. Section 522(d). However, each state also has its own exemption laws, and some states require you to use their exemptions instead of the federal ones.

Federal vs. State Exemptions

The bankruptcy system has a dual-track exemption structure. Section 522(b) gives each state the power to "opt out" of the federal exemptions. Here is how it works:

States That Allow a Choice

In roughly 17 states plus the District of Columbia, you can choose between the federal exemptions under Section 522(d) and the state's own exemption laws. This is significant because one system may be more generous than the other depending on your specific assets.

Opt-Out States

About 33 states have "opted out" of the federal exemptions. In these states, you must use the state exemption laws. You cannot use the federal list under Section 522(d). However, certain federal non-bankruptcy exemptions (like Social Security and veterans' benefits) still apply everywhere.

Key point: Even in opt-out states, some federal protections still apply. Social Security benefits, federal employee retirement, and veterans' benefits are protected by separate federal statutes that override state opt-outs.

The 730-Day Residency Rule

Under the Bankruptcy Abuse Prevention and Consumer Protection Act of 2005 (BAPCPA), you must use the exemptions of the state where you lived for the greater part of the 730 days (two years) before filing. If you moved between states during that window, you may be stuck using your former state's exemptions - or, if no state qualifies, the federal exemptions apply as a default under Section 522(b)(3).

This rule was designed to prevent "exemption shopping" - the practice of moving to a state with more generous exemptions (like Texas or Florida) right before filing bankruptcy.

The 1,215-Day Homestead Cap

Even if your state offers an unlimited homestead exemption, Section 522(p) caps the homestead at $189,050 if you acquired the property within 1,215 days (about 3 years and 4 months) before filing. This cap does not apply if you rolled over equity from a prior homestead in the same state.

Common Exemption Categories

Whether you use federal or state exemptions, most systems protect the same basic categories of property:

How Exemptions Work in Practice

Step 1: Determine Which System You Use

Check whether your state allows you to choose between federal and state exemptions. If your state has opted out, you must use the state system. Remember the 730-day residency rule if you have moved recently.

Step 2: List All Your Assets

On Schedule A/B of your bankruptcy petition, you must disclose every asset you own - real estate, vehicles, bank accounts, household goods, clothing, tools, investments, insurance policies, and any other property interest. Honest disclosure is required. Hiding assets is bankruptcy fraud.

Step 3: Claim Your Exemptions

On Schedule C, you claim the specific exemption that protects each asset. You cite either the federal statute (Section 522(d)) or the applicable state statute for each item. Strategic exemption planning is one of the most important services a bankruptcy attorney provides.

Step 4: The Trustee Reviews

In Chapter 7, the trustee examines your exemptions to determine whether any non-exempt equity exists. If everything is exempt, the trustee files a "no-asset report" and your case moves toward discharge. If you have non-exempt assets, the trustee may sell them - but you can sometimes negotiate to keep the property by paying the trustee the non-exempt value.

Good news: According to data from the Federal Judicial Center, roughly 95% of Chapter 7 cases are "no-asset" cases. Most people who file bankruptcy keep everything they own.

Exemptions and Chapter 13

In Chapter 13, exemptions work differently than in Chapter 7. You do not risk losing any property, because Chapter 13 is a repayment plan - not a liquidation. However, exemptions still matter because of the best interests of creditors test under Section 1325(a)(4).

This test requires that your plan pay unsecured creditors at least as much as they would have received in a Chapter 7 liquidation. So if you have $10,000 in non-exempt equity, your Chapter 13 plan must pay at least $10,000 to unsecured creditors over the life of the plan.

Higher exemptions mean lower required plan payments. That is why exemption planning matters even when you are filing Chapter 13 and keeping all your property.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep my house if I file bankruptcy?

In most cases, yes. The homestead exemption protects equity in your primary residence. The federal homestead exemption is $27,900, but many states offer higher amounts. Texas and a few other states provide unlimited homestead exemptions. If your equity is within the exemption limit, the trustee cannot sell your home. You must also stay current on mortgage payments.

Can I keep my car in bankruptcy?

Usually yes. The federal motor vehicle exemption protects up to $4,450 in equity. Many states offer higher amounts. If you owe more than the car is worth, there is no equity to protect and the exemption is not even needed. In Chapter 13, you keep all property and repay creditors through a plan. In Chapter 7, you may reaffirm the loan, redeem the vehicle, or surrender it.

Are retirement accounts protected in bankruptcy?

Yes. Most retirement accounts are fully protected. 401(k)s, 403(b)s, pensions, and other ERISA-qualified plans are exempt without any dollar limit under federal law. Traditional and Roth IRAs are exempt up to $1,512,350 (adjusted every 3 years). However, inherited IRAs are not protected after the Supreme Court's 2014 decision in Clark v. Rameker.

Can I keep my tools and equipment for work?

Yes. The federal exemption under 11 U.S.C. Section 522(d)(6) protects up to $2,800 in tools of the trade, including implements, professional books, and equipment used in your occupation. Many states offer higher amounts. Some states protect significantly more for farmers, mechanics, and other tradespeople.

What if I recently moved to a new state before filing bankruptcy?

Under BAPCPA (the 2005 bankruptcy reform law), you must use the exemptions from the state where you lived for the greater part of the 730 days (two years) before filing. If you moved within that window, you may be required to use your former state's exemptions. If no state's exemptions apply under this rule, you must use the federal exemptions regardless of whether your current state has opted out.

Foundational Caselaw

The cases below define the doctrine on this site:

  • Schwab v. Reilly, 560 U.S. 770 (2010) - when a debtor claims a specific dollar amount in exemption schedules, only that amount is exempt; the trustee can sell appreciated value above the claimed amount without filing a Section 522(l) objection. Choosing whether to claim "value" or "in full" controls the outcome.
  • Law v. Siegel, 571 U.S. 415 (2014) - bankruptcy courts may not surcharge exempt property to satisfy administrative expenses arising from the debtor's misconduct; the bankruptcy court's general equitable powers under Section 105(a) cannot override express provisions of the Code (here, Section 522(k)).
