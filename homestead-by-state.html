Homestead Exemption by State

How much home equity you can protect varies dramatically by state - from $0 to unlimited

Home › Homestead by State

How the Homestead Exemption Works

The homestead exemption protects equity in your primary residence from the bankruptcy trustee. "Equity" means the current market value of your home minus what you owe on mortgages and liens. If your equity is within the exemption limit, the trustee cannot force a sale of your home.

The amount of protection depends entirely on which state's exemptions you use. Some states offer unlimited homestead exemptions (subject to acreage limits), while others provide little or no homestead protection.

States with Unlimited Homestead Exemptions

These states place no dollar limit on the homestead exemption, though most impose acreage restrictions:

StateDollar LimitAcreage Limit
TexasUnlimited10 acres urban / 100 acres rural (200 for family)
FloridaUnlimited1/2 acre in municipality / 160 acres outside
KansasUnlimited1 acre in city / 160 acres farmland
IowaUnlimited1/2 acre in city / 40 acres outside
South DakotaUnlimited1 acre in town / 160 acres outside
OklahomaUnlimited1 acre urban / 160 acres rural

BAPCPA cap for recent movers: If you acquired your homestead within 1,215 days (about 3.3 years) before filing, the exemption is capped at $189,050 under 11 U.S.C. Section 522(p), regardless of state law. This prevents people from moving to an unlimited-exemption state and immediately filing bankruptcy to shield assets.

Federal homestead exemption (in states that allow it): $27,900 per filer, $55,800 for married couples filing jointly.

The 2-Year Domicile Rule

Under 11 U.S.C. Section 522(b)(3)(A), you must use the exemptions of the state where you were domiciled for the 730 days (2 years) immediately before filing. If you moved states during that period, you must use the exemptions of the state where you lived for the greater portion of the 180-day period before the 730-day period.

In plain English: if you recently moved from Missouri to Texas, you cannot immediately claim Texas's unlimited homestead exemption. You would still be stuck with Missouri's $15,000 limit until you have lived in Texas for at least 2 years.
