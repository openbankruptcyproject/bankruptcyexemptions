Wildcard Exemption Strategy

How to maximize the most flexible exemption in bankruptcy law

Home › Wildcard Strategy

What Is the Wildcard Exemption?

The federal wildcard exemption under 11 U.S.C. Section 522(d)(5) can be applied to any property -- cash, bank accounts, tax refunds, vehicles, legal claims, cryptocurrency, or anything else. It has two components:

  • Base amount: $1,475 that you can use regardless of other exemptions
  • Unused homestead bonus: Up to $13,950 of any unused portion of the homestead exemption under Section 522(d)(1)

Combined maximum: $15,425 per debtor. For a married couple filing jointly: $30,850.

Why Renters Have a Major Advantage

If you rent and do not own a home, you use $0 of the $27,900 homestead exemption. That means you can shift the full $13,950 into the wildcard, giving you $15,425 to protect any property you choose.

This is one of the most underused protections in bankruptcy. Many renters do not realize they have this much additional protection available. A single renter can protect $15,425 in bank accounts, tax refunds, or other property that would otherwise be vulnerable.

Example: A single renter with $8,000 in a savings account, a $3,000 tax refund, and a $4,000 car (paid off). Total non-exempt property: $15,000. Using the full wildcard ($15,425), everything is protected.

Homeowners: Partial Wildcard

If you own a home and use some of the homestead exemption, you can still shift any unused portion to the wildcard. For example, if your home equity is $10,000, you use $10,000 of the $27,900 homestead exemption. The remaining $17,900 in unused homestead is available -- but the wildcard cap limits the bonus to $13,950.

So even a homeowner with $10,000 in equity still gets the full $13,950 bonus plus the $1,475 base = $15,425 wildcard.

The wildcard bonus only starts shrinking when your home equity exceeds $13,950. At that point, the unused homestead portion drops below the cap.

State Wildcard Exemptions

Some states that have opted out of the federal exemptions offer their own wildcard:

  • California (System 2): $33,050 wildcard (no homestead in System 2)
  • Missouri: $600 general wildcard
  • Ohio: $1,450 general wildcard
  • Georgia: $1,200 wildcard per individual

In states that allow the federal exemption election, compare the state wildcard against the federal one. The federal wildcard is more generous in most situations, especially for renters.
